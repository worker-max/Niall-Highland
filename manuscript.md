Table for One: Fixing Your Service So I Can Finally Enjoy Dinner
How to Stop Losing Guests, Make More Money, and Give Me a Damn Break When I Eat Out

Introduction

I walk into restaurants the way some people walk into a crime scene. I’m not proud of it, and I’m not even trying to do it, but after twenty years in the industry, my brain won’t shut up. It’s narrating everything: the host stand is too far from the door, the menus are sticky, the server is in the weeds and doesn’t know it yet, table twelve just got their entrees and table ten doesn’t even have waters. I used to think noticing all of this made me a pain in the ass. Now I think it makes me honest. It also makes me tired.

I love restaurants in the way you love the friend who’s late but shows up with soup and a good excuse. I love the smell at four o’clock before the doors open, when the floor is clean and the lemons are sliced and everyone is still a person instead of a role. I love the hush before the first guests arrive. I love the chaos when the ticket machine wakes up. I love the little miracle of a table that’s having the exact kind of night they wanted. I love restaurant people, too. They are funny in a way no one else is funny, and they can read a room faster than a dog can find a dropped piece of steak.

The problem is that loving restaurants doesn’t make me blind. It makes me more awake. It’s like you can’t unhear a note once you know music, and you can’t unsee a clunky welcome once you’ve stood behind a host stand on a Saturday night. I’m not the only one. Anyone who’s ever carried three plates without a tray has a permanent radar for where the service seams are. The difference is, I decided to make that radar part of my life, which means I have to live with it. It follows me into date nights and birthday dinners and quiet lunches with my notebook open to a blank page, trying to be a normal person who doesn’t tally how long it takes to greet a table.

I started as a hostess in Littleton, New Hampshire, which is a lovely place to be sixteen and think the reservation book is the most powerful object in the building. The restaurant was a half-step from a diner, with vinyl booths and coffee that was always on. The town knew everyone’s business and everyone’s order. There was a woman who came in for soup every Friday and insisted on a lemon wedge and two extra crackers. There was a man who only wanted the corner booth and told the same story about his snowmobile every time. It was a slow education in people, and I learned two things quickly: first, nobody is a number, and second, if you treat them like a number, they remember.

From there I bartended, served, managed, opened places, closed places, did the midnight mop. I moved to Boston and learned the language of a fast-paced dining room, where you can seat four parties in the time it takes to say, “Right this way.” I learned how a bar feels different when it’s full of first dates versus playoff games. I learned that a server can change the temperature of a room with a smile. I learned that good hospitality can make people generous, and bad hospitality can make people small. Then I moved to Charleston and learned the language of slow-cooked hospitality, where you get credit for eye contact and a goodbye that feels like a blessing. Somewhere in between, I stopped being able to turn off the part of my brain that sees the seams.

People always ask if it’s hard to go out to eat when you’ve worked in restaurants your whole life. The answer is yes, and also, no. It’s hard because I see the tiny ways things can go wrong, and it’s hard because I know how much work it takes to make a night feel seamless. But it’s not hard because I’m not out there to punish anyone. I’m not a secret shopper. I’m not looking to be right. I’m looking to be taken care of the way I take care of other people. I want to sit down and be given the gift of letting go for an hour. I want to feel a little less in charge.

There are nights when it happens, when I walk into a place and the welcome is easy, the pacing is smooth, and the staff is calibrated to the room the way a good band is tuned to their audience. Those nights feel like taking a deep breath after a long drive. They remind me why this industry matters. They also make me want to climb behind the host stand at the places that miss the mark and say, in a voice that doesn’t terrify anyone, “Let me show you what I mean.”

This book isn’t a roast. It’s not a list of everything you’re doing wrong and a gold star for the ones who get it right. It’s a love letter and a gentle intervention. It’s the stuff I want to say to the people I love in this industry, without the alarm bells of a pre-shift pep talk or the exhaustion of a late-night debrief. It’s for servers and hosts and bartenders and managers and owners and anyone who has ever wanted a guest to leave feeling like a person again. It’s for the guest who wants to be seen and the worker who wants to be valued. I want that for you. I want it for me. I want to walk into your restaurant and be able to sit back, unclench, and finally enjoy dinner.

If this sounds like a manifesto, it isn’t. It’s a conversation. It’s a long one, told through the stories and lessons that only come from being in the trenches, getting your apron strings tied in knots, and still showing up because there’s something about service that feels like a calling even on the nights when it feels like a punchline. It’s about the moments that matter: the first hello, the first refill, the pause before dessert, the last thank you. It’s about the rhythm that holds a meal together and the kindness that people can feel without knowing how to name it.

I didn’t write this because I think I’m better than anyone. I wrote it because I’ve been almost everyone. I’ve been the hostess who was so nervous she forgot to smile, the server who missed a cue, the bartender who didn’t hear a guest call out, the manager who wanted to fix everything and couldn’t fix it fast enough. I’ve also been the guest who waited too long and the guest who got the wrong drink and the guest who spent the car ride home arguing about whether it was worth speaking up. This book is a bridge between those lives. It’s the space where I get to say, we can do better without burning out, and we can be better without pretending we’re perfect.

You’ll see me in here. You’ll see Littleton and Boston and Charleston. You’ll see the shift change, the pre-shift pep talk, the way a dining room hums when the team is in sync. You’ll also see the cracks, because that’s where the light gets in. The stories are true in the way real stories are true: the details are stitched together from a thousand nights and a thousand tables, but the feelings are exact. I’m not interested in dramatizing for the sake of drama. I’m interested in telling you what it’s like to stand on the floor and watch a night go from shaky to smooth with a few simple choices.

If you’re in this industry, you already know how hard it is to keep people happy while keeping yourself upright. If you’re not, I want to let you in on why that quick smile at the door is not a small thing, and why a server who remembers your drink is doing more than being polite. I want to show you the human side of the work without turning the book into a pity parade. We’re not asking for medals. We’re asking for enough support to do the job with dignity and enough awareness to make the guest feel like the point, not the obstacle.

So here’s the deal. I’m going to tell you what I see when I walk in, what I wish I didn’t see, and what I hope for when I sit down. I’m going to tell you about the moments that make a guest feel cared for and the moments that undo hours of good work. I’m going to do it with humor, because if you can’t laugh in a restaurant, you won’t last long, and with affection, because I still love this work with my whole heart. And if we do this right, by the end, we’ll all be a little better at the thing we say we’re here for: hospitality that people can feel.

Chapter One

The first time I said, “Just one?” I was sixteen and terrified and trying to keep my voice from cracking. I had a black button-down, a pen tucked behind my ear, and a reservation book that might as well have been the Constitution. The place was the kind of small-town restaurant where everyone was someone’s neighbor, and they believed they had a reservation because they knew the owner’s cousin. I thought I was being efficient, like if I moved fast enough no one would notice I had no idea what I was doing.

A woman in a navy coat walked in alone. She had the look of someone who came out to dinner not because she was lonely, but because she didn’t want to cook and she didn’t want to answer questions. I asked, “Just one?” the way I’d asked it a dozen times already. She paused, tilted her head, and said quietly, “It sounds like you’re disappointed I’m here.” It wasn’t mean. It wasn’t a scolding. It was just an honest observation that landed in me like a pebble in a shoe. It didn’t hurt, but I never stopped feeling it.

That sentence stayed with me in a way that surprised me. I replayed it the way you replay a song with a line you can’t shake. It wasn’t about her being sensitive or me being rude. It was about the vibe I had chosen without realizing I had chosen it. I sounded like I was bracing for something. I sounded like I was busy. I sounded like her arrival was a tiny complication. It was a small slip, but the thing about hospitality is that small slips are often the only thing a guest can feel. They’re not taking notes. They’re just feeling. They’re deciding if they’re wanted, and they decide quickly.

Years later in Boston, I watched a host with a jawline sharp enough to open an oyster call a couple to the stand and say, “Two? So… just two.” The couple smiled politely, the kind of smile people wear when they don’t want to be a problem. Their shoulders sank half an inch, and it was such a small movement that you would have missed it if you weren’t looking. But I saw it, and it bothered me, because I knew exactly how many little moments like that can turn a night into something you’d rather forget.

Boston taught me about speed, but it also taught me about dignity. In the rush of a Friday night, it’s easy to forget that the people walking in have chosen you. They could be anywhere. They picked your restaurant, your bar, your team. They might be celebrating or recovering. They might be on a first date or a last one. The only thing you know is that they trusted you with their time. And the first voice they hear is the host. That voice sets the tone like the first line of a novel.

I moved to Charleston because I needed the pace of my life to match the pace of my heart. I thought I’d leave behind the clenched jaw of Boston and live in a world of porches and yes ma’ams. What I found was a city that takes hospitality seriously, but also a city that can be seduced by its own charm. Sometimes a smile is there because it’s expected, not because it’s offered. Sometimes a “Welcome in!” is a line said out of habit, not a promise. The guest can tell the difference. The guest always knows.

If you’ve never worked a host stand, you might think it’s just a podium and a puzzle. But it’s not. It’s a little theater and a little triage. It’s a map of all the hopes and expectations walking through the door. It’s the place where you decide whether the night is going to feel easy or tense. It’s the place where you can take control of a chaotic evening by offering one calm sentence, or you can throw gasoline on it by letting your stress leak into your greeting.

There’s a particular kind of night that I think about a lot. It’s the night when the reservations are stacked, the walk-ins are piling up, and the kitchen has already gone off the rails. You can hear it in the printer, a steady stream of tickets that don’t pause. The manager is on the line, the bartenders are sprinting, and every server is double-sat. On that night, the host stand becomes the heart. It can either pump blood or it can seize. If the host collapses, the rest of the room will follow. If the host holds steady, the room can still breathe.

I’ve seen a host rescue a night with a sentence. There was a Saturday in Boston, the kind of night where everyone had decided at once that they wanted oysters. The wait list was long, the bar was three deep, and a couple walked in, clearly already unhappy. The host looked up, smiled, and said, “We’re going to take great care of you. Let me find you the right spot.” It was a simple line, but it changed the air. The couple softened. They sat at the bar. They ordered drinks. They tipped well. The entire night pivoted on a sentence that took five seconds to say.

I’ve also seen the opposite. I’ve seen a host who couldn’t stand the pressure and let it bleed into everything. The welcome was clipped. The eye contact was nonexistent. People felt dismissed before they even touched a menu. The servers spent the night trying to fix a first impression they didn’t create. The manager spent the night comping dishes for a problem that started at the door. None of it was about the food. It was about the tone.

This is what I want to say to hosts: your job is not to be perfect, it’s to be present. If you’re busy, say you’re busy, but say it like a person. If you need time, ask for it with confidence. The guest can handle a wait. What they can’t handle is feeling invisible or unwelcome. Acknowledge them. Use their name if you have it. Look up. The rest will follow.

There is something tender about being seated. You’re handing over time and money and expectation. You’re saying, take care of me for a little while. When the first words you hear feel like you’re an inconvenience, the spell breaks. It’s not a big tragedy, and it’s not a five-alarm fire. It’s just a small cut, and enough small cuts can bleed a restaurant dry.

I want to underline that this isn’t just a manners thing. It’s economics. A good welcome leads to a longer stay. A longer stay leads to another round, another appetizer, another indulgence. A guest who feels seen is a guest who tips well and comes back. If you’re fighting for every dollar, you should be fighting for the quality of the first thirty seconds. It is the cheapest, fastest, most reliable way to change the rest of the night.

The irony is that the host stand is often staffed by the least trained person in the room. We give the newest hire a podium and a tablet and tell them to smile. We don’t teach them the power they hold. We don’t teach them how to read a room, how to de-escalate with a sentence, how to keep their stress from spilling onto the guest. We should. If you want a smooth night, train the host like they’re the opening act, because they are.

I think back to that woman in the navy coat a lot. I never saw her again, but she taught me the difference between a question and a welcome. She taught me that a two-word phrase can shape a whole meal. She also taught me that most people aren’t trying to be difficult. They’re just trying to feel like they belong. That’s the heart of hospitality. It’s not the fancy specials or the linen napkins. It’s the simple act of making someone feel like there’s a place for them.

There was a night recently when I went out alone. I sat at a little bistro bar and waited for a seat. The host was busy, the dining room was full, and the takeout orders were stacked like a Jenga tower. She glanced up, made eye contact, and said, “Give me two minutes. I’ve got you.” She wasn’t bubbly. She wasn’t over-the-top. She was present. That sentence bought her patience. It bought her time. It bought my goodwill. Two minutes later, she slid me into a corner booth with a view of the street and said, “This one’s my favorite. I hope you like it.” I did. I ordered a glass of wine I didn’t need and a dessert I definitely didn’t need. I still think about that little moment when I need to remind myself that hospitality is not complicated, it’s human.

That’s the dream, really. To take someone by the hand without touching them. To make them feel like they’re exactly where they’re supposed to be. The host stand can do that, or it can do the opposite. That’s why I keep talking about it. It’s not glamorous, but it is the hinge of the night.

If there’s one thing I want you to take from this chapter, it’s that the first thirty seconds matter more than you think they do. The words you choose are small, but the message they send is big. You’re not just seating a party. You’re starting the show. And if you want the audience to lean in, you have to invite them. That invitation is simple, and it’s free, and it can change everything.

I wish I could tell you that hosts always have it easy, but they don’t. It’s a strange job. You’re the face of the restaurant and the traffic controller. You get blamed for kitchen delays you can’t control and for tables that linger too long. You are the first person a guest sees and the last person they remember if the night goes wrong. On a busy night, the host stand is a pressure cooker. That’s why the tone matters even more. When someone is stressed, their words go sharp. When someone is supported, their words soften. The guest can feel the difference from the doorway.

I remember a night in Littleton when we had a snowstorm and still somehow filled the dining room. People shook off their coats and stomped their boots and all wanted to be warm at once. I was flustered and short. An older man walked in and said, “Rough night?” and I admitted it was. He smiled and said, “You’re doing great.” That small kindness shifted me. I greeted the next table with a better smile. It made the whole room feel calmer. It was the first time I felt how contagious a single line could be, and it was the first time I realized the host doesn’t just manage a wait list, they manage a mood.

That’s the part most people don’t train. We teach hosts how to read the floor chart and how to quote a wait time. We don’t teach them how to read faces. We don’t teach them how to stand in the doorway and be both honest and optimistic. We don’t teach them how to keep their shoulders relaxed so the guests don’t carry their tension into the room. But those are the things that matter. That’s the difference between “We’re slammed” and “We’re busy, but we’ll take care of you.”

When I consult, I spend time at the host stand not because I want to micromanage, but because it’s where the story starts. I listen for language. I listen for the tone. I listen for the moment when a guest asks about the wait and the host answers with either a sigh or a smile. It is amazing how much of a night you can predict by that one response. A host who can say, “I hear you, we’re on it,” can save a Friday. A host who says, “I told you thirty minutes,” can lose a customer for good.

There is also a quiet dignity in the host stand that deserves respect. It is not a lesser job. It’s a frontline job. When you honor it, the whole restaurant gets better. When you ignore it, you spend the rest of the night putting out fires that started at the door. I’ve done both. I’ve ignored it because I was busy with a new menu or a staffing issue, and then I watched the room unravel. I’ve also leaned in, trained the host to lead with warmth, and watched the night smooth itself out.

That’s what I mean when I say the host stand isn’t a mood ring. It doesn’t have to reflect whatever chaos is happening behind the scenes. It can be its own calm. It can be the place where the guest feels held, even if the kitchen is behind and the bar is backed up. The host can be the person who says, “You’re going to have a good night,” and makes it true. That’s not fluff. That’s strategy. That’s how you stop losing guests before they ever sit down.

I’ve noticed that the way you handle the wait is often the difference between a guest who stays and a guest who leaves. A long wait is not a death sentence. A vague wait is. When a host says, “It’ll be a bit,” guests hear uncertainty. When a host says, “It’ll be about twenty minutes, and we’ll text you,” guests can plan. They can grab a drink, step outside, or settle into the bar. They feel in control. That feeling keeps them from walking out.

There’s also the question of space. The entryway is the first physical impression, and it matters. If the host stand is crammed into a corner with nowhere to wait, guests feel like they’re in the way. If there’s a clear, comfortable place to stand, they feel expected. You don’t need a grand lobby. You need a few feet of breathing room and someone who makes that space feel intentional.

I think about a restaurant in Boston that had a tiny foyer and a huge wait. The host would step out from behind the stand and meet people at the door, almost like a maître d’. He’d say, “Thanks for coming in. We’re about twenty minutes, but I’ll keep you updated.” That small movement made the space feel bigger. It made the wait feel organized. It made people feel chosen, not trapped. He took a cramped space and made it feel like a welcome.

Handling reservations is its own art. It’s not just about the book; it’s about the mood. When you tell someone their table isn’t ready, you have the chance to turn a potential problem into a moment of care. “We’re just putting the finishing touches on your table” feels different from “We’re running behind.” One says you’re preparing for them. The other says you’re struggling. It’s a small shift, but it changes how the guest feels while they wait.

There’s a solo dining moment that deserves special attention, too. The person dining alone is often the most vulnerable guest. They don’t have a companion to buffer the awkwardness. They notice everything. If you make them feel welcome, they become your biggest fans. If you make them feel like a burden, they’ll never come back. I’ve watched solo diners sit in the bar area even when they wanted a table because it felt less exposed. A host who says, “We have a great table for you,” can change that. They can turn loneliness into confidence.

The first step of service is also the first act of respect. It’s where you set the standard for the whole night. When you say, “Welcome in,” and you mean it, you make the guest feel like the night is worth their time. When you say it because it’s on a checklist, they can tell. People are more perceptive than we give them credit for. They feel the difference between a real greeting and a robotic one. The best part is that a real greeting doesn’t take any more time. It just takes intention.

I’ve watched hosts rescue a night by remembering a name. In Charleston, a host once greeted a couple with, “We’ve got your table ready,” even though their reservation wasn’t in the system. They weren’t trying to game the list; they had simply called ahead and the note was missed. The host could have said no and sent them away. Instead, she said yes and made a tiny adjustment to fit them in. They left glowing, and they told their friends. That kind of flexibility is a form of hospitality that doesn’t show up in a training manual, but it builds loyalty in a way no marketing campaign can.

The host stand is also where you set the expectation for the pace. If you tell a guest you’ll be right with them and you’re not, you’ve lost trust. If you’re honest about a wait and keep them updated, they’ll forgive the delay. This is a place where small promises are everything. A guest will forgive a long wait if you keep your word, but they won’t forgive being ignored. That’s the difference between a full dining room and an empty one the next weekend.

I know some hosts who keep a tiny notebook with quick notes: a couple’s anniversary, a guest’s favorite table, a regular’s name. It’s not fancy. It’s just a way of saying, we see you. Those notes become the difference between service that feels generic and service that feels personal. If you want to stand out, remember a detail. It doesn’t have to be big. It just has to be real.

Chapter Two

The best server I ever worked with in Boston used to arrive at the pass like a stage manager on opening night. She didn’t rush, but she didn’t drift. She’d take a second to scan her section, then she’d glide to the table that had been waiting the longest. She didn’t need to check her watch; she could feel the clock. She was the first one to understand that timing isn’t just logistics, it’s mood. If you show up too fast, you’re interrupting. If you show up too late, you’re apologizing. If you show up right on time, you feel like magic.

I learned this the hard way on a night when I was triple-sat and had convinced myself that water was optional. I greeted table fifteen with menus and a smile and said I’d be right back with waters. In my head, I was moving at light speed. In reality, I got pulled into a drink order, a table needed forks, and a couple asked for recommendations. By the time I returned, the guest at table fifteen had been waiting long enough to leave a faint ring on the menu with their own condensation. They didn’t complain. They just looked at me the way people look at a bus that’s already passed them by. The rest of the night never quite recovered.

Water isn’t just water. It’s a signal. It says, you’re safe here. You’re being taken care of. It buys you time and buys the guest calm. I know this because I’ve been the guest who sits at a table, scans the room, and makes a quiet calculation about whether I’m going to have to fight for every refill. The second someone drops a glass and says, “I’ve got you,” my shoulders drop. It is a small thing that feels like a big thing. That’s hospitality.

I’ve watched this play out again and again, and I’ve seen the way a tiny delay can create a ripple. If you don’t show up in time, the guest starts making decisions without you. They decide the cocktail isn’t worth asking about. They decide they’ll just get water. They decide they don’t want to be a problem and they’ll keep their questions to themselves. You have lost the chance to guide their night. It doesn’t feel dramatic. It feels like a slow leak. That’s the kind of leak that drains revenue without anyone noticing.

In Charleston, there’s a restaurant where the servers train for the greeting like it’s choreography. The greeting is short and sweet, and it always includes eye contact and water on the first touch. No one says it out loud, but you can feel the difference. People relax. They settle. They feel the rhythm. That place stays busy not because the food is a miracle, but because the people feel cared for.

The other side of timing is how you check in. There’s a difference between a check-in that feels like a helicopter and a check-in that feels like a hand on your shoulder. The first makes the guest feel watched. The second makes the guest feel seen. I used to say, “Still working on that?” because I heard it a thousand times and thought it was a normal thing to say. Then I heard myself say it to a couple who clearly didn’t love their entrees, and I saw the way their faces fell. “Still working on that?” sounded like a chore. It sounded like a task I was waiting to be done. I could have said, “How’s everything tasting?” and it would have been true and kind and not at all complicated.

I keep a list in my head of tiny phrases that land wrong, not because they’re malicious, but because they’re thoughtless. The words come out of your mouth and you realize too late that they didn’t feel good. This happens to all of us. The solution is not to memorize a script, it’s to be mindful of how the guest might hear you. They’re not inside your head. They’re inside their night.

When I think about water and eye contact, I think about the whole emotional arc of a meal. There’s the moment you walk in and hope to be welcomed. There’s the moment you sit down and hope to be noticed. There’s the moment you place your order and hope to be understood. Water is the simplest sign that those hopes are being met. Eye contact is the other. If you don’t look at someone, you’re not really talking to them, you’re talking past them. It’s easy to forget that when you’re in the weeds, but a guest doesn’t care that you’re in the weeds. They care that you’re there.

There was a night when I was serving in Boston and a regular came in with his dad. The dad was older and moved slowly, and the regular had the kind of anxious smile that told me this dinner mattered. I greeted them quickly, dropped water, and then made sure to check in with the dad first when I came back. It wasn’t a grand gesture. It was just a small shift in attention. The regular caught my eye and nodded, and I knew it was the right thing. That night taught me that timing and attention can be a kind of kindness, and kindness is contagious. The dad left smiling. The regular left a note on the check that said, “Thank you for seeing him.”

There’s a rhythm to service that guests can feel, even if they can’t name it. It’s the rhythm of showing up at the right time, of not interrupting, of giving the guest space and then gently closing the gap. When you get it right, the whole night feels easy. When you miss it, the night feels like a series of small stumbles.

Sometimes I hear managers say, “We’re a fast-paced place,” as if speed is the same as care. Speed can help, but speed without timing just feels like being rushed. You can drop food fast and still leave the guest feeling unseen. You can be busy and still be present. The goal is not to be the fastest, it’s to be the most aware.

If I could give every server one tiny superpower, it would be the ability to scan the room the way a parent scans a playground. You’re not hovering, you’re not anxious, you’re just aware. You know who needs you and who doesn’t. You know when to approach and when to step back. That awareness is the difference between service that feels smooth and service that feels like a chase.

The funny thing is that most guests are forgiving. They know you’re human. They know you’re juggling. But they still want to feel like the meal is theirs, not yours. Water, eye contact, and timing are the easiest ways to give them that feeling. They cost you nothing and they pay you back all night.

I still hear that Boston server in my head when I walk a floor. She’d glance at a table that just sat and say, “Give them a minute.” Then, when a different table was ready for a check, she’d say, “Now.” She didn’t say it out loud. She just knew. That’s what I want for every server who reads this. Not a script. Not a list. A feel. A rhythm. A quiet confidence that says, I’m here, I’m watching, I’ve got you.

There’s a kind of nervous energy that new servers carry, and it often shows up in the greeting. They rush because they’re anxious. They over-apologize because they feel behind. They narrate the wait instead of guiding it. It’s understandable. I did it, too. I remember a night when I approached a four-top and said, “Sorry, sorry, sorry, I’m so sorry, I’m new.” I meant to be honest, but it made the table nervous. They spent the rest of the meal bracing for mistakes that never came. The lesson wasn’t to pretend I was perfect; it was to be calm enough that they could be calm. Water, eye contact, and a steady voice would have done more than any apology.

Sometimes timing is about knowing when not to say anything. A guest takes their first bite and closes their eyes for a second. That’s not the moment to ask if everything is okay. That’s the moment to step back and let them have the bite. Timing is about letting people enjoy the quiet, and not filling every space with a question. The best service feels like a rhythm, not a speech.

I’ve also learned that water refills are emotional currency in a way that surprises people who don’t work in restaurants. A full glass makes a guest feel cared for. An empty glass makes them feel forgotten. If you’re busy, a refill can buy you patience. It’s the cheapest way to build goodwill. And it doesn’t have to be dramatic. You can top off a glass as you pass, make brief eye contact, and you’ve just said, “I see you.” The guest feels it.

One of my Charleston regulars used to say, “I’m not asking for a hug, I’m asking for a refill.” It was a joke, but it wasn’t. She wanted to feel noticed.
