I want to talk about a specific mistake I see boards making, and I want to be careful about how I say it because the people making it are doing it for very good reasons.

The mistake is writing the AI policy first.

Here is what that looks like in practice. A board chair, very reasonably, sits down with the head of school and says: parents are asking us about AI. The inspectorate is asking us about AI. We need a position. We need a document. We need something we can hand to a prospective family and say, this is what we believe and this is what we do. So the head writes a policy. Or the head asks a small committee to write a policy. The committee meets four times. They produce a thoughtful document. The document goes to the board. The board approves it. The document gets circulated to faculty. And then nothing happens.

Nothing happens because the policy is describing a practice that doesn't exist yet. The teachers reading the policy have not used AI. The teachers reading the policy do not know what acceptable use looks like, what unacceptable use looks like, what the assessment implications are, what the differentiation opportunities are. The policy is asking them to enforce a set of distinctions they have not yet had to make in their own work. They will, very politely, file the document and not change anything they do.

The schools that are getting this right are inverting the sequence. They are doing practice first, language second, policy last.

Practice first means giving teachers six to twelve weeks of structured, low-stakes, supported time to actually use AI in their planning. Not workshops. Not inset days. Real teachers, doing real work, using AI to help, with a colleague to compare notes with at the end of the week. After six to twelve weeks of this, teachers have something they didn't have before: opinions. They have specific opinions about what worked, what didn't, where the line is between assistance and replacement, what they want their students to be able to do without AI and what they think it's fine to use AI for.

Language second means convening the teachers who now have those opinions and asking them to write down what they're hearing in their own departments. Not write a policy. Write a description of practice. What does fluent AI use look like in our science department this term. What does it look like in our English department. The descriptions will not match. That's the point. Those gaps are where your real policy questions are.

Policy last means taking those descriptions of practice and asking, three months in: what does the school as a whole need to commit to? Now, finally, you write the policy. And the policy you write is one that your teachers can implement, because it describes what they're already doing.

I want to give you a concrete example. A school in Singapore I worked with last year ran exactly this loop. They had a draft AI policy in February. They threw it out. They put fifteen teachers through a fluency program from March to June. In September, they reconvened the teachers and asked them to write department-level practice descriptions. In November, they used those descriptions to draft a school-wide policy. In December, the board approved it. The whole process took ten months. The policy they ended up with was one-third the length of the one they had thrown out. And the teachers in the school can recite the relevant sections from memory, because they wrote it.

Policies written before fluency are brittle. Policies written after fluency are durable. If your school is in the document-first phase right now, my strong suggestion is to slow down, build practice, and write the document after you actually know what you're describing. The schools who do this in 2024 will have policy in place by mid-2025 that survives the next three years of model improvements. The schools who write the policy now will be rewriting it twice in the next eighteen months.

Slow is fast, here. Practice is the input.

Thank you.
